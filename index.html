How the scan works

From a map click to a verdict, in four steps.

01 — LOCATE

Pick a point

Click anywhere on the map, or jump to one of ten known high-risk sites like Saqqara or Amarna.

02 — CAPTURE

Fetch the patch

The backend requests a fresh 256×256px satellite image centered on that point from Mapbox.

03 — INFER

Run the ensemble

Five ResNet-50 models, each trained on a different fold, vote on whether the patch shows looting pits.

04 — REPORT

Get a verdict

A Looted / Not Looted call with confidence scores, using a threshold tuned to minimize missed sites.

The problem

A crime scene the size of a country, checked one satellite image at a time.

In the security vacuum following Egypt's 2011 revolution, looting of archaeological sites surged sharply. Looters dig shafts and trenches straight into unexcavated ground, searching for antiquities to sell — leaving behind a signature that satellite imagery can't hide: clusters of dark, irregular pits scarring the desert floor around known and undiscovered sites.

Egypt has thousands of archaeological sites and only a fraction of the personnel needed to physically monitor them. Heritage authorities and researchers can watch satellite feeds, but doing it manually — scrolling through Google Earth tile by tile — doesn't scale. This project asks a narrower, answerable question: can a model trained on confirmed looting imagery flag new pits automatically, fast enough to matter?

Dataset & methodology

Built from confirmed sites, not synthetic data.

Sourcing

Every image patch was captured from Google Earth Pro's historical imagery archive, spanning 2010–2016 — the period covering the sharpest rise in illegal excavation. Historical imagery lets the same location be compared before and after known looting events.

Locations

Focused on three of Egypt's most heavily documented and most heavily looted necropolis regions: Dahshur, Lisht, and Saqqara — sites with enough confirmed cases to build a reliable positive class.

Labels

508 labeled patches split into two classes — Looted (clear evidence of excavation pits) and Not Looted (pristine terrain or natural desert features) — published openly on Kaggle for reuse.

Architecture

A 5-fold ensemble of ResNet-50 classifiers. Each fold trains on a different split of the data; at inference time all five vote, which smooths out the variance any single model would carry from a dataset this size.

Threshold tuning

The default 0.5 decision boundary was deliberately moved to 0.4007, chosen to reduce false negatives — in site protection, a missed looting event is a worse outcome than a false alarm worth double-checking.

Validation

Evaluated with out-of-fold cross-validation across 504 held-out samples, so every reported number reflects performance on data each fold never trained on.
